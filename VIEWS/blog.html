Snap, Nap, or Tap: Jiu Jitsu As A Metaphor for Web Development

As a relatively recent convert to Brazilian Jiu Jitsu, as well as a coding rookie, I have come to draw and appreciate the parallels between the two arts. Because, trust that they're both art forms.

There are even parallels in how I came to both disciplines - quite by accident. I was living in San Diego, which I did not know happens to be the world capital of Brazilian Jiu Jitsu (outside of Rio De Janiero, of course). If you toss a stick in San Diego, chances are you'll hit a jiu jitsu practitioner (I would NOT recommend doing this, however). My wife's bestie happens to be a brown belt, and through numerous chats with him, my curiosity got the better of me. Next thing I knew, I was being tossed around on the mat like a rag doll by a skinny 15-year-old whippersnapper with entirely too much upper body strength for my liking. I somehow fell in love with the brutality of it all, and have been a convert ever since.

Software development came to me in pretty much the same manner - suddenly, sometimes brutally - but with enough glimpses into the sheer beauty of it to make me fall head over heels with it. And so now, here I am, furiously tappity-tapping away at my keyboard, inputting spells and incantations one keystroke at a time, hoping to coax out and carve out a little beauty in my tiny corner of the interwebs.

Other parallels include...

1: Understanding the concept of Heirarchy as a tool to not get my butt kicked. As a white belt I would never think to challenge my brown belt friend, for example. And similarly, finally understanding CSS specificity rules have saved me a ton of frustration.

2: Some days you're the dog... and some days you're the hydrant. It's easy to get ahead of yourself when you achieve a small win or successfully apply a new technique that you weren't able to before. What happens next? You get thrown a curveball - either a bug or a black belt - that has you questioning the sanity in your choices. Because, balance. The important takeaway is: remain humble, or someone (or some code) is going to do that humbling for you.

3: Tenacity beats technique, every single time. On the mat, when I'm tangled up in a ridiculous hold, the key is to: not panick; take deep breaths, and visualize step by step the specific body movements that will help me to get un-stuck. The same with software development: when stuck, learning the art of rubber ducking; being able to describe to yourself how to break down a seemingly monumental task into bite-sized chunks; and having the clarity and patience to remain steadfast, will always yield better results than panicking ever will.
